Tips & Tricks: Encounter Design

The easiest adventures to prepare for are ones you write for yourself, because you can be absolutely sure you’ll know how it works. But one part of writing your own adventure can be particularly annoying: designing encounters which have the right level of difficulty. Fortunately, Pathfinder has you somewhat covered with the Challenge Rating system. Rather than going over that system in detail, let me just give you the basics of how I would design an encounter from scratch.

The first thing I would do is decide how challenging I want the encounter to be. I would look up the party’s level on the table below, cross-reference with the column corresponding to the desired encounter difficulty, and read off my XP budget. This is the XP value of monsters per character I need to add to make the encounter of the appopriate difficulty.

To give an example, suppose I have a party of five 3rd-level characters. I want them to face a challenging encounter. I go to the "XP Budget per Character" table, read on the row for party level 3 and the column for a “Challenging” encounter. I see that I have a budget of 400 XP per character, or 2000 XP total. I would then go through the bestiary and look to add about 2000 XP worth of monsters.

Continuing our example, I want 2000 total XP. I might use a CR 3 monster and a CR 4 monster working together.

A few notes that might help…

  • An “average” encounter is one which should deplete about ¼ of the party’s relevant daily resources, and is the sort of thing you should expect the group to be able to handle without too much difficulty.
  • If you go to the d20pfsrd, you can find a list of monsters rated by CR.
  • It is better to have more weak monsters than one strong monster, because the party’s advantage in numbers means they can usually defeat single strong monsters pretty easily.
  • Don’t be afraid to use the environment to help make the encounter harder. Fog might make it difficult to see. A monster might place itself in a doorway to limit the number of opponents who can attack it at one time. These kinds of things can make an encounter harder and more memorable.

What this table means by NPC class or PC class is whether the opponent uses NPC classes (warrior, adept, or so on) or PC classes (fighter, wizard, etc.).

The wealth is a little funny, but what I mean is this:

  • If you have "PC Wealth" read from the PC column. Incidentally, try to place enough loot so that our characters will be near this benchmark.
  • If you have "NPC Wealth" but use a PC class, read from the "NPC (PC Class)" column.
  • If you have "NPC Wealth" and use an NPC class, read from the "NPC (NPC Class)" column.

Let's return to our example of five 3rd-level characters facing a challenging encounter. Remember the XP budget was 2000 XP. I decide that they should face other characters, rather than monsters.

From the table, I see that two 3rd-level and two 2nd-level characters with PC classes and NPC wealth match my budget of 2000 XP. I might thus put them against two 3rd-level fighters with 1650 gp in gear each, and two 2nd-level rogues with 780 gp in gear each. This is an encounter I expect the party to win, but they'll take some heavy blows.

A useful place to look for help in making NPCs is here. What it calls "Basic NPCs" are NPCs with NPC class levels, while "Heroic NPCs" are NPCs with PC class levels. Also helpful are this page for Heroic NPCs and this page for Basic NPCs. These have pregenerated NPCs you can just look up.

I decide that I don't want to deal with creating my two 2nd-level and two-3rd level NPCs. I go to the page for Heroic NPCs and I decide on a standard party of four. I pick a “Traitorous Bastard” fighter (600 XP), a “Skulking Brute” rogue (400 XP), a “War Priest” cleric (400 XP), and a “Battle Mage” wizard (600 XP). These all have skills, feats, and gear already set.
